GCD and LCM

Greatest Common Divisor (GCD)

The Greatest Common Divisor (GCD) of two or more integers, which are not all zero, is the largest positive integer that divides each of the integers without a remainder. For example, the GCD of 8 and 12 is 4.

Properties of GCD

  • The GCD of two numbers is the largest number that divides both of them without leaving a remainder.
  • The GCD of two prime numbers is always 1.
  • The GCD can be calculated using the Euclidean algorithm.

Euclidean Algorithm

The Euclidean algorithm is an efficient method for computing the GCD of two numbers, based on the principle that the GCD of two numbers does not change if the larger number is replaced by its difference with the smaller number.

Least Common Multiple (LCM)

The Least Common Multiple (LCM) of two or more integers is the smallest positive integer that is divisible by each of the integers. For example, the LCM of 4 and 5 is 20.

Properties of LCM

  • The LCM of two numbers is the smallest number that is a multiple of both.
  • The LCM of two prime numbers is their product.
  • The LCM can be found using the relationship between GCD and LCM.

Relationship between GCD and LCM

LCM and HCF of Two Numbers

For any two positive integers, a and b, the relationship between their LCM and HCF is given by:

LCM(a, b) × HCF(a, b) = a × b

This means that the product of the LCM and HCF of two numbers is equal to the product of the numbers themselves.

LCM and HCF of Three Numbers

For three positive integers, a, b, and c, the relationship extends by considering pairwise calculations:

LCM(a, b, c) × HCF(a, b, c) = LCM(LCM(a, b), c) × HCF(HCF(a, b), c)

This formula builds on the pairwise relationship to account for three numbers.

LCM and HCF of Four Numbers

For four positive integers, a, b, c, and d, the relationship is extended similarly:

LCM(a, b, c, d) × HCF(a, b, c, d) = LCM(LCM(LCM(a, b), c), d) × HCF(HCF(HCF(a, b), c), d)

This iterative approach applies to four numbers by continuously using the results of previous pairs.

LCM and HCF of Five Numbers

For five positive integers, a, b, c, d, and e, the relationship can be generalized as:

LCM(a, b, c, d, e) × HCF(a, b, c, d, e) = LCM(LCM(LCM(LCM(a, b), c), d), e) × HCF(HCF(HCF(HCF(a, b), c), d), e)

This approach is consistent with the pairwise method applied to five numbers.

LCM and HCF of Six Numbers

For six positive integers, a, b, c, d, e, and f, the relationship follows the same iterative process:

LCM(a, b, c, d, e, f) × HCF(a, b, c, d, e, f) = LCM(LCM(LCM(LCM(LCM(a, b), c), d), e), f) × HCF(HCF(HCF(HCF(HCF(a, b), c), d), e), f)

This method generalizes the pairwise relationship to six numbers.

Conclusion

The relationship between LCM and HCF for two numbers is direct and straightforward, while for more than two numbers, it involves an iterative process of pairwise calculations. Understanding these relationships helps in solving various problems involving multiples and factors in mathematics.
