Fermat's Last Theorem

Introduction to Fermat's Last Theorem

Fermat's Last Theorem states that no three positive integers a, b, and c can satisfy the equation an + bn = cn for any integer value of n greater than 2.

Theorem Statement

Fermat's Last Theorem, formulated by Pierre de Fermat in the 17th century, asserts that:

There are no positive integers a, b, and c such that an + bn = cn for any integer value of n greater than 2.

This theorem remained unproven for over three centuries until Andrew Wiles famously provided a proof in 1994.

Historical Context

Pierre de Fermat, a French mathematician, first wrote about his conjecture in the margin of his copy of Arithmetica by Diophantus in 1637. He claimed to have a proof but did not provide it. For centuries, mathematicians attempted to prove or disprove Fermat's conjecture, leading to significant developments in number theory.

Implications and Legacy

Wiles' proof of Fermat's Last Theorem has had a profound impact on mathematics, demonstrating the power of modern mathematical techniques and opening new avenues of research in algebraic geometry, number theory, and related fields.

Related Conjectures

Fermat's Last Theorem is part of a broader class of conjectures related to equations of the form an + bn = cn for other values of n. While Fermat's original conjecture was specific to n > 2, similar equations for other values of n remain open questions in number theory.
