Algebraic Number Theory

Introduction to Algebraic Number Theory

Algebraic number theory is a branch of number theory that studies the arithmetic properties of algebraic numbers, which are roots of polynomial equations with integer coefficients. It explores the structure of number fields, their rings of integers, and extends concepts like prime factorization to ideals in these fields.

Theorems and Results

Number Fields and Rings of Integers

Number fields are finite extensions of the rational numbers, often denoted as (K). Each number field ( K ) has a corresponding ring of integers (mathcal{O}_K ), which generalizes the concept of integers to these fields.

Ideal Theory

Ideal theory in algebraic number theory extends the concept of prime factorization from integers to ideals within the ring of integers ( mathcal{O}_K ). The fundamental theorem of ideal theory states that every nonzero ideal in ( mathcal{O}_K ) can be factored uniquely into a product of prime ideals.

Class Field Theory

Class field theory connects number fields to abelian extensions, which are Galois extensions with abelian Galois groups. It establishes a deep relationship between number fields and ideal class groups through the use of ideles and the Artin reciprocity map.

Axioms and Methods

Algebraic number theory employs various axioms and methods to explore the properties of number fields and their rings of integers:

  • Norms and Traces: These functions on elements of number fields play a crucial role in defining the arithmetic behavior of these fields and their extensions.
  • Discriminants and Ramification: The discriminant measures the extent of "ramification" or complication of the field extension at prime ideals.
  • Minkowski Bound: Provides an upper limit on the absolute value of the discriminant of a number field in terms of its degree and regulator.

Applications

Algebraic number theory finds applications in various areas of mathematics and beyond:

  • Diophantine Equations: Uses algebraic methods to study equations with integer solutions, such as the study of elliptic curves over number fields.
  • Class Number and Units: Computes the class number of a number field, which measures the failure of unique factorization, and studies units in number fields.
  • Cryptography: Certain cryptographic protocols rely on the properties of number fields and their rings of integers for security, such as those based on ideal lattices.

Future Directions

The future of algebraic number theory involves exploring more complex relationships and developing computational tools:

  • Non-abelian Class Field Theory: Extending class field theory to non-abelian Galois extensions to understand more intricate relationships between number fields and their arithmetic properties.
  • Computational Aspects: Developing efficient algorithms for computing with number fields, including algorithms for computing class groups and units.
